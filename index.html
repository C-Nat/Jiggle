What exists today

Why many current snacks still don’t work

There are already protein jellies, sweet puddings, and ward snacks. But many options miss the moment people actually need help: low appetite, dry mouth, sore mouth, and taste changes (like a metallic taste).

Current protein jelly drink

Brittle & artificial
  • Brittle jelly texture that can break into chunks instead of sliding smoothly.
  • Artificial flavour and aftertaste that become obvious with low appetite.
  • High sugar profile that feels more like candy than a gentle nutrition drink.

Gelatin dessert cups

High sugar
  • Sugar packed jelly that leans heavy into sweetness.
  • Dessert-style flavour that can be tiring or nauseating during chemo.
  • Not designed for dry mouth, sore mouth, or metallic taste changes.

Luxury biscuits & dry ward snacks

Dry & effortful
  • Dry texture that can scratch and worsen mouth dryness or soreness.
  • Crumbly biscuit that takes effort to chew and swallow.
  • Often low protein, mainly offering calories instead of targeted nutrition.

Real-world references: a current protein jelly drink (brittle, artificial, sugary), a gelatin dessert cup, and a luxury biscuit often seen in wards — all helpful to see what Jiggle is trying to do differently.

Why jelly, not biscuits?

Made for dry mouth, soreness & taste changes

Chemo can make everything taste wrong. Mouths feel dry, sore, and every bite of a biscuit feels like work. Jiggle is built as a small kindness for those days when you still need protein, but chewing is the last thing you want to do.

Soft, slippery texture
Jelly drink slides instead of scraping. Less friction on sore mouths and throats.
Flavours that cut through chemo taste
Bright lemon salt and gentle coconut are chosen to be refreshing, not overpowering, to work around distorted taste without feeling artificial.
Small, meaningful serving
100 g is enough to matter nutritionally, but small enough to finish even on low-appetite days, whether in hospital or at home.

Jiggle is a concept product designed to explore a kinder, high-protein snack for athletes, everyday life and people with low appetite. Not medical advice: final formulations should always be checked with healthcare teams and dietitians for safety and suitability.
